The two grounds

Not a dark theme. A dark object.

Most kiosk software picks one surface and stays there. Ours alternates, because the product does: the customer touches a black instrument and walks away holding warm paper. Screens that stage the experience use the instrument ground. Screens that show the artwork use the paper ground — so the print always looks like a print, never like a UI element.

Instrument

Cool near-black, biased slightly toward violet so the gold lamp reads warm against it. Machined edges, one indicator light, nothing decorative.

Color

One lamp, two grounds.

The accent is a single warm gold — the indicator light on a piece of photographic equipment. It is the only saturated hue in the interface chrome, which is what makes it read as premium rather than busy. Spend boldness in one place; keep everything around it quiet.

Series hues are assigned in this fixed order and never cycled — color follows the entity, so filtering a chart never repaints the survivors. This ordering was chosen by running every gold-leading permutation through colorblindness simulation and keeping only those that passed both grounds: worst adjacent separation ΔE 9.4 on the instrument and 9.2 on paper, against a target of 8.

Typography

Monospace is the voice, not the caption.

Most systems bury mono in code blocks. Here it leads — because a receipt is monospaced, and that is what the brand already sounded like. Set tight and large it reads as precision equipment; set small with wide tracking it reads as instrument labelling. A neutral grotesque carries running text so the mono never has to be comfortable, only characterful.

Elevation is one light source, high and front: a long soft shadow downward plus a 1px inner highlight along the top edge. That top highlight is what makes a dark panel read as a machined object instead of a flat rectangle — it is the single most valuable pixel in the system.

Motion

Every transition answers a question.

Motion here is not decoration — it is the kiosk explaining itself to someone who has never used it and will use it once. Each curve has a job: where did this come from, is the machine working, did my tap register. Tap any curve to replay it.

Duration scale

Duration scales with distance and consequence, never with importance. A chip toggling is 80 ms because it moved 2 px. A kiosk screen change is 600 ms because the whole surface moved and the customer must follow it. Anything past 600 ms must be a deliberate ceremony — the countdown, the flash, the print emerging — or it just feels slow.

Choreography — stagger, don't swarm
40 ms between siblings, capped at 8

Items enter in reading order, 40 ms apart, and stop staggering after the eighth — past that the delay is longer than anyone will wait. Exits never stagger. Leaving is uniform and fast, because nobody wants to watch a screen they have already dismissed.

The four rules
RuleWhy
Transform & opacity onlyThe only two properties the GPU animates for free. Animating width, top or filter drops a fanless tablet to single-digit frame rates mid-session.
Motion has an originA panel opened from the top-right corner enters from the top-right. Motion without an origin reads as noise.
Interrupt, never queueA customer tapping twice must not wait through two animations. Every transition is cancellable and reversible from wherever it is.
Ceremony is earnedOnly three moments get more than 600 ms — countdown, shutter flash, print emerging. They are the product. Everything else gets out of the way.
Components

Press feedback is a 3.5% scale-down over 140 ms. On a touchscreen with no hover, the press is the affordance — it is the only confirmation a guest gets that the glass heard them.

Kiosk ergonomics

Designed for one use, by a stranger, in bad light.

ConstraintRule
Viewing distanceBody text never below 15px; primary actions never below 48px tall. A guest stands further from a kiosk than from a laptop.
No hoverNo hover state may carry meaning anywhere. Every affordance is visible at rest — hover is a bonus on the laptop build only.
Thumb reachPrimary actions sit in the lower third of the portrait screen. The header is for orientation, never for controls.
One decision per screenTapping a choice advances immediately. No choose-then-confirm — it doubles the taps and halves the throughput at a busy event.
Venue lightingBody text holds 7:1 against its ground, not 4.5:1. Booths run in dim receptions and blown-out daylight, sometimes in the same hour.
Wet, cold, gloved handsNothing depends on a precise gesture. Pinch and rotate always have a handle-drag equivalent.
The honest constraint

A fanless tablet is also rendering your print.

This is the tension in the brief worth naming. The same device running these animations is compositing a 1200×1800 print, running Sharp, writing SQLite and serving a local HTTP server — and it has no fan. A luxurious system that throttles the machine is not luxurious; it is a booth that stutters in front of a paying customer.

BudgetRule
Compositor onlytransform and opacity. Nothing else animates, ever.
Blur is rationedAt most one backdrop-filter layer on screen. It is the most expensive effect in the system and the easiest to over-use.
Nothing loops idleExcept the attract screen, which is the one moment the machine has nothing else to do.
Render yieldsPrint composition and animation never run in the same frame — the print render is queued behind the transition, not raced against it.
Reduced motion is realprefers-reduced-motion collapses every duration to near-zero and disables the attract loop. State still changes; it just stops moving.

The kiosk ships with a performance tier. If frame time exceeds budget for a sustained window, the shell drops blur, then parallax, then stagger — in that order — and keeps the core transitions. The booth degrades gracefully instead of stuttering proudly.
